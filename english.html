Specimen No. 01
Scotland

Haggis

United Kingdom · Scotland
Ingredients Used

The heart, liver, and lungs of a sheep, mutton fat, oatmeal, and spices. It is traditionally served with "neeps and tats" (turnips and potatoes).

black pepper allspice nutmeg mace
Preparation Procedure

All the sheep parts are ground up and mixed together with the mutton fat, oatmeal, and spices. This mixture is then packed tightly inside a sheep's stomach and boiled for a few hours.

Reason for Strangeness

It uses animal organs ("the parts of a sheep that no one else wants") and is cooked inside an actual animal stomach.

Specimen No. 02
Japan

Shiokara

Japan
Ingredients Used

Raw squid and salt.

Preparation Procedure

Raw squid slices are mixed thoroughly with a salty, fermented paste made out of the squid's own belly/internal organs. The raw liver is taken out, mashed up, and mixed with a very large amount of salt. The mixture is sealed in a container for a few weeks to ferment. During this time, the natural enzymes in the liver break down the raw squid meat, turning it into a strong, salty, and pungent delicacy.

Reason for Strangeness

It consists of completely raw seafood mixed into a heavily salted, fermented animal stomach lining, which gives it an intense, challenging flavor that even some locals take time to get used to.

Specimen No. 03
China

Jellyfish Salad

China
Ingredients Used

Jellyfish, chicken, and sesame oil.

Preparation Procedure

The jellyfish is sliced into a rubbery, stringy texture, mixed into fresh salads, paired up with shredded chicken, and stirred completely through with rich sesame oil. It is traditionally seasoned with garlic, soy sauce, and a pinch of sugar or chili flakes to balance the taste.

Reason for Strangeness

People usually associate jellyfish with their scary, stinging nature in the ocean rather than seeing them as a low-fat, rubbery salad ingredient.

Specimen No. 04
Australia

Green Ants

Australia
Ingredients

Whole green ants, often paired with goat's cheese and a native herb called lemon myrtle.

Preparation

These little insects are pulled straight from tree branches, lightly crushed between the hands, and eaten raw.

Strangeness

Eating whole insects is a massive psychological challenge for many, yet they surprise consumers by naturally tasting remarkably lemony and peppery without any added spices!
