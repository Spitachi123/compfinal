Three Castes of the Hive

Every honey bee colony is a superorganism built on division of labour

👑

Queen Bee

Fertile Female · 1 per colony
  • Sole egg-laying female
  • Lives 2–5 years
  • 32 chromosomes
  • Lays ~3000 eggs/day
  • Produces pheromones
  • Mates once in her life
♂

Drone Bee

Haploid Male · 500 per colony
  • Sole purpose: mating
  • Lives ~2 months
  • 16 chromosomes
  • No stinger or venom gland
  • Larger head & eyes
  • Dies after mating
⚙

Worker Bee

Sterile Female · 20,000–80,000
  • Performs all hive tasks
  • Lives 6 weeks–6 months
  • 32 chromosomes
  • Has pollen baskets
  • Collects nectar & pollen
  • Structurally unique
Apis mellifera Queen Bee
Caste 01

The Queen Bee

Fertile Female 1 per colony Diploid · 32 chromosomes

The queen is the largest single fertile female bee in a honey bee colony. Her body is divided into head, thorax and abdomen. Her head is smaller than other bees and she has a shorter proboscis. Queen bees have smoother stingers with smaller barbs, allowing her to sting more than once.

Eggs per day
~3,000
Lifespan
2–5 years
Chromosomes
32 (diploid)
Mates
Once only
Reproduction The queen mates only once and stores sperm in a spermatheca in her abdomen, using it throughout her life. A diploid cell divides into haploid cells with 16 chromosomes during gamete formation.
Egg Types The queen lays both diploid and haploid eggs. Diploid eggs become worker bees or another queen. Haploid eggs develop into drones (males).
🧠 Memory Tip The pheromone produced by the queen bee attracts drones for mating. It also makes worker bees infertile.
Apis mellifera Drone Bee
Caste 02

The Drone Bee

Haploid Male ~500 per colony 16 chromosomes

Drone bees are the fertile and haploid male bees. Their number ranges from a few hundred to 500 in a hive. Their body is divided into head, thorax and abdomen. They have larger head and eyes than queen bees. Generally, drones are very lazy and do not work.

Population
up to 500
Lifespan
~2 months
Chromosomes
16 (haploid)
Stinger
None
Origin: Parthenogenesis Drones are formed from haploid (unfertilised) eggs. This process is called parthenogenesis — they contain only half the number of chromosomes (16) in their cells.
Fate after Mating Drones die after mating because during mating the tissues in the abdomen tear apart. If winter approaches, they are expelled from the hive by worker bees or killed.
🧠 Memory Tip Drone bees cannot eat nectar by themselves because they have very small proboscis and lack chewing parts in the mouth. They depend entirely on worker bees for food.
Apis mellifera Worker Bee
Caste 03

The Worker Bee

Sterile Female 20,000–80,000 per colony 32 chromosomes

Worker bees are sterile females that perform all the jobs in the hive. They are the smallest bees, structurally different from the queen and drones. They have 3 pairs of legs with hairs and special mouth parts for chewing and lapping to collect nectar and pollen.

Population
20K–80K
Lifespan
6 wk–6 mo
Chromosomes
32 (diploid)
Pollen Baskets
Yes
Pollen Basket Workers have special hairs and sac-like structures in their feet called the pollen basket (corbiculae). They use these to store and carry pollen grains to the hive to feed larvae, the queen and drones.
Royal Jelly Worker bees feed a special liquid called royal jelly to selected larvae. A female larva continuously fed with royal jelly turns into a queen.
🧠 Memory Tip If a worker bee loses its pollen basket, it cannot collect pollen grains. So it cannot help in pollination, causing agricultural production to decline and the forest ecosystem to become imbalanced.
